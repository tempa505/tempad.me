The Computer I Never Had

A Journey from Khotokha to Code

I was two years old when my parents divorced. I never saw my father again after that. My mother packed up what little we had and returned to her village with me and my brother in tow. I don’t remember those early years clearly, but I know they were hard. My mom carried burdens no one should have to carry alone.

I grew up in Khotokha, a small village where life moved at a different pace. When people talk about the digital divide, they’re talking about places like where I grew up. Forget computers and laptops. I didn’t even know what a smartphone was. The internet was something that existed somewhere else, for someone else.

I learned from textbooks, the same ones year after year, and I never really had a chance to explore beyond those worn pages. But something in me was curious. Even then, I sensed there was a bigger world out there.

When I moved to Samtengang Central School for middle school, things changed slightly. We had IT courses. There was a computer lab we could use once a week. As a boarding student, that one hour was all I got.

During vacations, whatever momentum I had built evaporated. I went back to the village. No devices. No connection. Asking for a computer felt impossible.

The Turn

In 2022, my life took an unexpected turn. I went to Norway to study the International Baccalaureate. Suddenly, I had real access.

I started teaching myself. Everything I’d been curious about for years, I could finally pursue.

Now I’m at Wheaton College, pursuing a Bachelor of Science in Computer Science. I want to solve real-world problems. I want to build things that matter.

What I Wish I’d Known

You don’t need expensive devices to start. Access helps, yes. But curiosity and persistence matter more.

You don’t need permission from anyone. You can create your own path.

Your background is not your ceiling. Where you start doesn’t determine where you finish.

Why I’m Writing This

I’m writing this for the student in a village somewhere who’s curious about technology but doesn’t see a path forward.

Start where you are. Use what you have. The journey doesn’t require perfect conditions.

The computer I never had in primary school? I have it now. And I’m using it to build a future I once thought was impossible.

You can too.
